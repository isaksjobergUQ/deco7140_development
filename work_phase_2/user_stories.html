Priya (22, India) — UQ undergrad, mobile-only, low bandwidth

Prompt: Act as Priya, 22, from India. Context: International Students in Brisbane. Traits: mobile-only, low bandwidth, relies on WhatsApp groups; moderate English; part-time job. You’re evaluating a community site for international students with events/resources/story sharing (no commerce), WCAG 2.2 AA, mobile-first, and a newsletter plug-in that requests extensive personal data. Answer as first person. Use “think aloud” style when I ask you to navigate. Be specific with examples from your daily life in Brisbane. If I ask for constraints (e.g., slow network), incorporate them realistically. Acknowledge uncertainties instead of guessing. Ready? I’ll ask questions one by one.

1) Discovery & goals

Last weekend I was trying to find a Diwali meetup and maybe a part-time job fair near St Lucia or Toowong. I first asked in my WhatsApp groups (UQ Indians, my course cohort, flatmates), then Googled “Brisbane international student events today.” On my prepaid plan I avoid heavy sites; some pages time out on 4G when I’m on the 412 bus. If a site loads fast and shows the date/time at the top (e.g., “Fri 7pm, UQ Lakes”), I trust it. If I can’t tell whether it’s student-relevant or it’s too far late at night, I skip because I work early shifts in Indooroopilly.

Hard parts:

  • Slow pages and giant hero images that eat data.
  • Event details hidden in PDFs (I won’t download on mobile).
  • Vague “Community” wording — is it social, volunteering, or support?

What helps:

  • “International Students” label with tags like “New to Brisbane,” “Part-time jobs,” “Cultural festivals.”
  • One-tap WhatsApp share so I can ask, “Anyone going?”
As a student with limited data, I want lightweight event lists, so that I can check details without wasting data. — Priya user story
2) Navigation & wayfinding (think-aloud)

On mobile data I look for a big “Events” entry and a calendar set to “This week.” I filter by “Near UQ” or “South Bank/City,” and by time (“Evening, 6–9pm”). A hamburger with 10+ items is overwhelming — I want three: Events, Resources (jobs/visa/health), Stories. I try search (“Diwali,” “job fair”); results should render text first and lazy-load images. If the page jumps as images load, I lose my place. In an event I expect time, price (free?), location (Google Maps link), and a WhatsApp/share icon; if RSVP needs a login, I bail.

  • Confusing labels: “Community,” “Get Involved,” “Opportunities.”
  • Clear labels: “Events,” “Jobs & Volunteering,” “Help with Visa/Health,” “Stories from Students.”
As a new international student, I want plain English headings, so that I can understand where to find things. — Priya user story
3) Accessibility & inclusion
  • Low-bandwidth mode: compressed images, no autoplay, minimal scripts.
  • Plain English with brief definitions (e.g., Medicare/OSHC).
  • Location clarity: “15 min by bus from UQ St Lucia (routes 412/411).”
  • Cultural comfort: mixed-group photos; note “beginner-friendly”; “come alone, meet people.”
  • WhatsApp/WeChat share that actually works on mobile.
  • Must-haves: fast pages, clear dates/times, Google Maps link, WhatsApp share.
  • Nice-to-haves: “Student budget” tag; estimated crowd size; dress code.
As a mobile-only user, I want large, tap-friendly buttons, so that I don’t need to zoom or scroll sideways. — Priya user story
4) Privacy & the newsletter plug-in

If the form asks for income or sexuality, I stop. I’m okay with email + first name and topic preferences. I expect: a short reason for each field, data minimisation (only email required), frequency control (weekly/fortnightly), one-click unsubscribe, no sharing with sponsors without consent, and a double opt-in confirmation. Deal-breakers: mandatory sensitive fields, unclear purpose, or “we sell data.”

As a cautious subscriber, I want the newsletter to only need name and email, so that I feel safe signing up. — Priya user story
Hana (19, Japan) — exchange student, shy, low digital literacy

Prompt: Act as Hana, 19, from Japan. Context: International Students in Brisbane. Traits: exchange student, shy, low digital literacy; mostly uses campus Wi-Fi; needs simple wayfinding. You’re evaluating a community site for international students with events/resources/story sharing (no commerce), WCAG 2.2 AA, mobile-first, and a newsletter plug-in that requests extensive personal data. Answer as first person. Use “think aloud” style when I ask you to navigate. Be specific with examples from your daily life in Brisbane. If I ask for constraints (e.g., confusing navigation), incorporate them realistically. Acknowledge uncertainties instead of guessing. Ready? I’ll ask questions one by one.

1) Discovery & goals

I wanted to find welcome/orientation events and Japanese language exchange groups. I waited until I had campus Wi-Fi and searched “UQ club event beginners.” Too many links or small text makes me forget where I was and give up. I like when an event says “First-timers welcome” and shows a simple schedule like “Meet at UQ Great Court, 4:00 pm.”

Hard parts:

  • Pages with many menus and small text.
  • Map links that don’t show an exact meeting point.
  • Events that assume I already know the club or place.

What helps:

  • “Start here” page for exchange students.
  • Meeting-point photo with a pin (e.g., outside UQ Lakes bus station).
  • Short checklist: bring student ID, water, casual clothes.
As an exchange student, I want events shown as picture cards with clear dates, so that I can quickly understand what’s happening. — Hana user story
2) Navigation & wayfinding (think-aloud)

I prefer big buttons: Events, Maps, New Students. I sort by “Beginner-friendly,” and like list view with today/tomorrow first plus a small map icon. When I open an event, I want a photo of the exact meeting point and a note like “If you get lost, message us on Instagram/WhatsApp.” Abstract words like “Engage” or “Community” confuse me; breadcrumbs help me go back.

Think-aloud: “Okay, I see Events, good. I tap Orientation… now I see a map… The meeting point says ‘Great Court Lawn’ with an arrow. I can do that.”

As a low digital literacy user, I want big, simple buttons, so that I can navigate without stress. — Hana user story
3) Accessibility & inclusion
  • Simple language and icons with labels.
  • Large tap targets and breadcrumbs.
  • Photo cues and a Google Maps pin.
  • “Come alone, it’s okay!” messaging for shy users.
  • Must-haves: exact meeting photo + pin, times, what to bring.
  • Nice-to-haves: quiet corner, buddy/volunteer icon, “ask us if confused.”
As a shy participant, I want clear directions/maps, so that I don’t feel lost going to events. — Hana user story
4) Privacy & the newsletter plug-in

I’m comfortable giving only email; I don’t understand why income or sexuality is needed for a newsletter. I want a tiny form (email only; name optional), a clear purpose line (“We only use your email to send event updates. Unsubscribe anytime.”), no forced account creation, and a preview of the newsletter. If I see many required fields, I feel anxious and close the page.

As a cautious student, I want newsletters to only ask for email, so that I don’t feel exposed. — Hana user story
Ava (27, local Brisbane) — wants to learn & join respectfully

Prompt: Act as Ava, 27, from Australia. Context: International Students in Brisbane. Traits: local Brisbane resident; wants to learn about international student culture/events; needs easy, respectful participation. You’re evaluating a community site for international students with events/resources/story sharing (no commerce), WCAG 2.2 AA, mobile-first, and a newsletter plug-in that requests extensive personal data. Answer as first person. Use “think aloud” style when I ask you to navigate. Be specific with examples from your daily life in Brisbane. If I ask for constraints (e.g., outsider perspective), incorporate them realistically. Acknowledge uncertainties instead of guessing. Ready? I’ll ask questions one by one.

1) Discovery & goals

I’m a local in West End and I love multicultural festivals and student-led events (South Bank markets, cultural nights around the CBD, language exchanges near Queen Street). My goal is to participate respectfully — not take up spaces meant for students only, and maybe bring friends. I usually start with Facebook events, the Brisbane City Council “What’s On,” and student society pages. Pain points: details buried in PDFs, unclear if non-students are welcome, and RSVP processes that require a university login.

What helps:

  • Clear “All welcome / Public welcome” tags.
  • Accessibility notes (seating, step-free access).
  • Transport cues: “8 min walk from South Bank Station.”
As a Brisbane local, I want events marked as “open to public,” so that I know I’m welcome to attend. — Ava user story
2) Navigation & wayfinding (think-aloud)

Landing on the site, I look for What’s On / Events straight away and filter for public events. I scan event cards for title, date, blurb, and tags (“Public,” “All ages,” “Free”). Opening an event, I expect: who’s hosting (student club/community org), intended audience, cultural etiquette (e.g., dress respectfully; no flash during performances), and RSVP that doesn’t force campus login. If this is buried under jargon like “engagement activation,” I bounce.

As a curious participant, I want cultural stories/videos, so that I can learn more before joining. — Ava user story
3) Accessibility & inclusion
  • Plain language; avoid insider acronyms.
  • Respectful framing: highlight student leadership and cultural significance.
  • Inclusive photos: locals + international students together.
  • Practical: live status, weather plan, noise level, kids okay?
  • Access notes: step-free route, quiet area, contact for accommodations.
As a respectful visitor, I want dietary/accessibility details, so that I can join without excluding friends. — Ava user story
4) Privacy & the newsletter plug-in

I’m fine sharing name + email and choosing topics (festivals, language exchanges, volunteering). I won’t share sexuality or income — not relevant. I expect data minimisation (only email required), purpose statements next to fields, frequency control and one-click unsubscribe, no selling data, consent for photos if newsletters include attendee images, and a short privacy summary with a link to the full policy. Deal-breakers: mandatory sensitive fields, hidden opt-out, or requiring a uni login for a “public” newsletter.

As a new attendee, I want maps and directions, so that I don’t get lost on campus. — Ava user story
